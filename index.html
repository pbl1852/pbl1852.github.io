01 · Introduction

Why This Analysis Matters

StreamKUS is a new media company preparing to release its first original content on YouTube and Netflix. Since audience preferences change quickly, relying only on intuition can be risky. Therefore, this project uses a data-driven approach to identify what kinds of genres, themes, and story elements appeal to viewers.

For this report, our team analyzed eight popular contents across four genre categories: romance, thriller/crime, human and social documentary drama, and comedy. The selected contents were Single’s Inferno and When Life Gives You Tangerines for romance, Squid Game and Parasyte for thriller, There Are No Bad Dogs in the World and The Trauma Code for slice of life, and SNL Korea and Shortbox for comedy. By comparing viewer reactions to these works, we aimed to discover patterns that can guide StreamKUS’s future content planning.

Genres Covered

We picked 4 different genres: romance, slice of life, thriller, and comedy, and picked 2 shows from each genre to analyze. The reason we divided our analysis targets into genres was because we would get a better understanding of what appeals more to what audience, and for what reason. We picked these 4 genres because they are historically among the most popular genres, and we thought that this would help us get a better idea for planning content.

Dramas: We used the most watched Netflix shows from 2024-25 from each genre: 1 romance, 1 slice of life, and 3 thriller. For Korean reviews, we used Watchapedia (pedia.watcha.com), and for English reviews, we used Rotten Tomatoes (rottentomatoes.com). For each show, we used Codex to collect all user reviews available without post filtering, and piled them into a CSV file.

Reality: We used 1 romance, 1 slice of life, and 2 comedy reality shows that gained traction in recent years, aired on Netflix, TV, YouTube, and Coupang Play respectively. We collected YouTube comments and posts on social media sites Nate (nate.com) and DCInside (dcinside.com), and piled them into a CSV file using Codex for analysis.

For dramas, we used audience reviews, not critic reviews, as we thought that the overall reviews of a wider audience would be more fit for our needs than the more professional critic reviews. The same thought process went into collecting reality show reviews, as we thought YouTube comments as well as social media posts would be able to represent a wider audience, therefore leading to a better understanding of what the wider audience yearns.

03 · Text Mining Methods

Analytical Techniques Used

Method 01

Sentiment Analysis

We collected the sentiment analysis of the collected text using Colab.

Method 02

AI-Assisted Review Analysis

Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite Preview was used to generate review summaries, key themes, positive points, negative points, and keyword lists.

Method 03

Genre-Based Strategy

We compared the main positive and negative points for each genre and show to identify what StreamKUS should do and avoid.

We collected the sentiment analysis of the collected text using Colab, and with Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite Preview we used the following prompt:

"당신은 영화/TV 평론가이자 데이터 분석가입니다.전체 리뷰 중 {sample_size}개를 무작위로 추출한 데이터를 바탕으로 종합적인 리뷰 분석을 작성해 주세요.요구사항:**주의사항: 모든 분석 결과와 응답값은 반드시 '한국어(Korean)'로 작성해 주세요.**. 이 작품에 대한 사람들의 반응과 점수 분석 근거를 한국어로 명확하게 작성하세요. 긍정적 피드백, 부정적 피드백, 반복되는 주제를 배열 형태로 매우 상세하게 요약하세요. **중요**: 각 항목은 반드시 '**주제:** 설명' 형식으로 작성하세요. (예시: '**세계관의 확장:** 단순히 게임을 반복하는 것이 아니라...'). 샘플링된 리뷰들에서 가장 많이 언급된 핵심 키워드 상위 10개를 추출하고, 해당 키워드가 샘플 리뷰 내에서 대략 몇 번 언급되었는지 빈도수를 추정하여 포함하세요. 결론을 한국어로 작성하세요."

The result printed the overall sentiment score out of 10, the frequency of each number, main positive and negative points, and the most frequently mentioned topics and keywords.

Because our end goal is proposing content for our streaming platform, we analyzed the main positive and negative points for each genre/show and used them to pinpoint what we need to do and what to avoid when making a show for each genre.

04 · Findings

What Viewers Responded To

💗
Target: Women in their 10s, 20s, and 40s

Romance

This genre appealed most to women in their 10s, 20s, and 40s. For dramas, a good cast, good characters, a touching storyline, and good videography were the positives that appeared most often. However, unnecessary scenes and an incomplete plot were frequently discussed as negative points.

For reality shows, the cast made the biggest difference, along with the chemistry between participants. Long scenes and poor editing were the main complaints.

🎭
Target: Men 20s, 30s, 50+ / Women 40s

Slice of Life

This genre appealed most to men in their 20s, 30s, and 50s or above, and women in their 40s. For dramas, a good plot, good characters, and a clear message were key positive factors. Unrealistic scenes were the main complaint.

For reality shows, educational aspects and touching scenes were the main positives. However, repetitive plots made some viewers feel bored. The main talking point was the message the content wanted to deliver.

🔍
Target: Men in their 10s, 20s, 40s, and 50+

Thriller

This genre appealed most to men in their 10s, 20s, 40s, and 50s or above. A creative storyline, good acting, and strong videography were the most frequently mentioned positive points.

However, illogical story consistency and underwhelming endings were the main complaints. These shows were also often compared to their predecessors or other similar titles.

😄
Target: Men 40s, 50+ / Women 40s

Comedy

This genre appealed most to men in their 40s and 50s or above, and women in their 40s. Easily understandable and relatable topics, along with social satire, made comedy shows more appealing.

However, unrealistic or exaggerated points, generalization, and too much political intent made these shows harder to watch. Comedy shows tended to have very mixed reviews, with various opinions coexisting.

05 · Strategic Recommendations

Strategic Recommendations for StreamKUS

1

When The Seasons Stay

우리의 계절은 아직 · Romance · 6-Part Netflix Series
Genre: Romance Target: Women in their 10s, 20s, and 40s Format: 6-Part Netflix Series

StreamKUS proposes “When the Seasons Stay” as its first original emotional romance and family healing drama series. The story follows people who reconnect with love, family, and forgotten relationships while navigating the emotional changes of adulthood.

While many romance dramas focus mainly on excitement, confession, or dramatic heartbreak, this series explores the quieter emotions that remain afterward. Love is portrayed not as a perfect fantasy, but as something built slowly through understanding, forgiveness, sacrifice, and shared everyday moments.

The core of this content is not dramatic plot twists, but emotional realism found in ordinary life. Family dinners, old photographs, rainy conversations, silent misunderstandings, and small acts of care become emotionally meaningful moments that viewers can deeply relate to.

The series focuses on emotional humanism, showing how loneliness, regret, family conflict, and lost relationships can slowly heal through sincere connection with others. Through warm cinematography, soft seasonal visuals, emotional background music, and realistic dialogue, the drama creates a comforting atmosphere that reflects the emotional style audiences often associate with Korean romance dramas.

Rather than telling a story about perfect people, “When the Seasons Stay” portrays imperfect individuals learning how to remain beside each other despite emotional scars and changing seasons in life.

Episodes

  1. Ep.1: The Place I Left Behind (다시 돌아온 계절) — Returning home and facing old memories
  2. Ep.2: Things We Never Said (우리의 침묵) — Emotional distance within family
  3. Ep.3: That Rain That Day (비가 내리던 날) — Reuniting with a first love
  4. Ep.4: Dinner For Four (저녁 한 끼의 온기) — Family warmth hidden in everyday life
  5. Ep.5: After The Flowers Fall (꽃이 진 자리에) — Loss, regret, and emotional healing
  6. Ep.6: When The Seasons Stay (우리의 계절은 아직) — Accepting love, family, and moving forward

Reasons based on collected data: Text-mining analysis revealed that viewers strongly respond to the emotional humanism that is characteristic of Korean romance dramas. Audience reactions frequently included keywords such as cried, love, family, and touching, indicating that viewers value emotionally sincere stories about relationships, family bonds, healing, and meaningful human connections.

2

The Second Golden Time

두 번째 골든타임 · Slice of Life · Netflix Series + Short-Form Content
Genre: Slice of Life Format: 6-Part Netflix Series with Short-Form Content

The Second Golden Time is a human and social documentary drama that focuses on what happens after a patient survives an emergency. Each episode begins with an urgent medical case, such as a traffic accident, workplace injury, or emergency room crisis. However, instead of ending with a successful surgery, the story follows the patient’s second crisis after survival: medical bills, lack of caregivers, trauma, unemployment, family conflict, and social isolation.

The title “second golden time” refers to the crucial period after treatment, when a patient needs support to return to normal life. By focusing on this stage, the show can provide both the tension and catharsis that viewers enjoyed in The Trauma Code, while also delivering a more realistic and empathetic social message.

Episodes

  1. Ep.1: Alive But Not — A lone elder has nowhere to go after discharge
  2. Ep.2: Discharge Faster Than Money — A delivery driver is discharged after an accident, but has no choice but to return to the road
  3. Ep.3: Lone Wolf — A successful surgery but no one to help leaves patients lifeless
  4. Ep.4: Alive At What Cost — A patient lives miraculously, but the family has no money to pay the hospital
  5. Ep.5: Eerie at the ER — A deep dive into a family member who looked like a nuisance at the beginning
  6. Ep.6: 72 Hours After Discharge — Following a patient for 72 hours after discharge and showing how healthcare and community support should be connected

Reasons based on collected data: This proposal is based on our finding that viewers of The Trauma Code responded positively to fast pacing, charismatic characters, team chemistry, cathartic moments, and criticism of the medical system. Keywords such as fast-paced, characters, satisfying, medical system, and teamwork appeared repeatedly. However, viewers also criticized exaggerated scenes, unrealistic hero-like protagonists, and predictable medical drama clichés. Therefore, The Second Golden Time keeps the strengths of The Trauma Code, such as tension, teamwork, and social criticism, but avoids excessive hero fantasy by focusing on more realistic problems after emergency treatment.

3

Dead Chamber

죽음의 룰렛 · Thriller · 6-Part Netflix Series
Genre: Thriller Target: Men in their 10s, 20s, 40s, 50s and above Format: 6-Part Netflix Series

StreamKUS proposes “Dead Chamber” as its first original psychological survival thriller series. The story follows six strangers who are invited to participate in a mysterious roulette game where each spin determines life or death.

While many survival thrillers focus mainly on violence and action, this series emphasizes psychological fear, human instinct, betrayal, and moral conflict. The suspense comes not only from the deadly games themselves, but from the emotional pressure created when ordinary people are forced to make impossible choices to survive.

The core of this content is not simply death, but fear and uncertainty. The roulette wheel symbolizes fate, randomness, and desperation. As the game progresses, trust collapses between the participants, hidden secrets are revealed, and viewers begin questioning whether survival can still preserve someone’s humanity.

The series is designed to maximize immersion through dark cinematic visuals, CG-enhanced game sequences, rapid editing, intense sound design, and claustrophobic camera work. Rather than relying only on shocking scenes, the story builds tension through psychological warfare and unpredictable twists that encourage viewers to continue watching and discussing theories online.

Episodes

  1. Ep.1: The Invitation (죽음의 초대) — Six strangers enter a mysterious roulette game
  2. Ep.2: The First Spin (첫번째 룰렛) — The players start the game, but come to a deadly realization
  3. Ep.3: Trust No One (의심의 시작) — Alliances form, while suspicion grows
  4. Ep.4: Loaded Chamber (조작된 게임) — Hidden rules are revealed and players are manipulated
  5. Ep.5: The Last Bet (최후의 선택) — Betrayal destroys the remaining survivors
  6. Ep.6: Dead Chamber (죽음의 룰렛) — The truth behind the game is uncovered

Reasons based on collected data: According to recent popularity rankings on Netflix, survival thrillers have consistently attracted strong audience attention, as shown by the global success of series such as Squid Game and Parasyte: The Grey. Text-mining analysis of viewer reviews on Watcha and Rotten Tomatoes also revealed that keywords such as immersion, visual excitement, video quality, and CG appeared frequently in thriller-related discussions. These findings suggest that audiences strongly value visually engaging experiences, cinematic tension, and high-quality production elements within the thriller genre.

4

We’re Not Done Yet

우리 아직 안 끝났어 · Comedy · YouTube + Netflix Expansion Potential
Genre: Comedy Target: 20s and 30s with high media usage Format: YouTube Upload with Netflix Expansion Potential

This show follows a couple who have gotten married after seven years of dating, but run into realistic fights and laughs as newlyweds.

Unlike existing shows which focus on the talking, dating, breakup, and marriage stage, this show focuses on life after marriage. It wants to show that marriage is not a happy ending but a start of something new, as the couple run into unexpected situations after becoming husband and wife.

This show is not centered around dramatic scenes, but instead is centered around aspects of daily life such as going on dates, housewarming, in-laws, money, and much more, which are situations that many viewers have come across before or will come across. This show aims to shed light on these situations in a comedic manner.

Episodes

  1. Ep.1: Do We Need Dates? We’re Married! (결혼했는데 왜 데이트해야 돼?) — On the boundaries of dating and marriage
  2. Ep.2: The Housewarming War (신혼집 집들이가 전쟁이 되는 법) — Friends, housewarming, and how we look
  3. Ep.3: Your Mother, Not Mine! (양가 단톡방 답장은 누가 해?) — In-law problems
  4. Ep.4: Your Money? Our Money? (우리 돈이야, 내 돈이야?) — Money management problems
  5. Ep.5: You’re So Stuck In The Past (너 아직도 군대 얘기해?) — Repetitive talk about past experiences and problems
  6. Ep.6: You’ve Got Company (회사 사람 앞 내 배우자) — Social life after marriage

Reasons based on collected data: After analyzing YouTube comments and social media posts about Shortbox, we concluded that viewers reacted better to comedy when it is based on relatable aspects of life. There were also many requests for an expansion on this series, along with mentions of keywords such as newlywed, military service, work, and marriage. This was the reason behind the format allowing for expansion. If the series expands, viewer comments can be used to decide the topic of future episodes, helping the show avoid repetition and better match viewer wants and needs. Since some comments reacted negatively with words such as boring, repetitive, and stop, we chose life after marriage as a relatively less common topic.

06 · Limitations

Research Constraints

⚠️

Limited Data Scope

The data came mainly from publicly available reviews, YouTube comments, and online community posts. These comments do not represent all viewers because people who post online are usually more active, emotional, or opinionated than silent viewers.

⚠️

Platform Differences

The amount and type of data differed depending on the content and platform. Some works had many YouTube comments, while others had more review-site data or community discussions, which made direct comparison difficult.

⚠️

Text Mining Limits

Slang, memes, sarcasm, abbreviations, swear words, and community-specific expressions are difficult for AI tools to understand accurately. Because of this, some audience reactions may have been misclassified or not fully reflected in the analysis.

07 · Conclusion

Key Takeaways

This project aimed to help StreamKUS plan its first original content through a data-driven approach rather than relying only on intuition. By analyzing audience reviews, YouTube comments, and online community discussions across romance, thriller, slice of life, and comedy, we identified several patterns in what viewers respond to most strongly.

Overall, the analysis showed that viewers are attracted to content with clear emotional appeal, strong characters, relatable situations, and memorable story elements. In romance, viewers responded positively to emotional storytelling, family relationships, healing, and visually warm scenes, while they reacted negatively to unnecessary scenes or incomplete plots. In slice of life content, viewers valued meaningful messages, realistic characters, educational elements, and emotional sincerity, but disliked repetitive or unrealistic storytelling. In thriller content, audiences were drawn to creative storylines, strong acting, suspense, visual quality, and immersive tension, while weak logic or disappointing endings caused negative reactions. In comedy, viewers enjoyed relatable everyday topics, character-based humor, satire, and social commentary, but were critical of exaggerated settings, forced humor, excessive political intent, or repetitive formats.

Based on these findings, we proposed four content ideas for StreamKUS. “When The Seasons Stay” was designed as an emotional romance and healing drama based on the strong viewer preference for family, love, touching stories, and emotional humanism. “The Second Golden Time” was proposed as a human and social medical drama that keeps the urgency and teamwork audiences enjoyed in The Trauma Code, while focusing more realistically on patients’ lives after treatment. “Dead Chamber” was created as a psychological survival thriller based on the popularity of suspense, visual immersion, high production quality, and moral conflict in thriller content. Finally, “We’re Not Done Yet” was proposed as a relatable comedy about life after marriage, based on audience reactions to Shortbox and SNL that showed strong interest in everyday humor, relationship comedy, satire, and expandable short-form formats.

If more time and data were available, this project could be expanded by collecting more data from additional platforms such as TikTok, Instagram, X, Reddit, blogs, and more Korean online communities. Future research could also analyze audience reactions by demographic group, such as age, gender, country, or platform, to understand more precisely which viewers prefer which genres and story elements.

In conclusion, this project shows that audience data can provide useful direction for content strategy. While data cannot completely replace creative judgment, it can help identify what viewers value, what they criticize, and what kinds of stories are more likely to attract attention. For a new media company like StreamKUS, combining data-based insight with creative storytelling can reduce risk and lead to more audience-centered content planning.
